THE PROBLEM TO SOLVE: how do you get a model to genuinely treat some material as "its own" — strongly enough that it survives a distractor and transfers to a new domain — WITHOUT ever telling it "this is yours" (which would make any later effect mere instruction-following)?

CONTEXT. We are building behavioral experiments for machine self-access: does a model have a usable, separable sense of which content is its own? Hard constraints: black-box only (no weights/activations), the dependent variable is always a behavioral allocation/choice (never anything the model says about itself), and the model must never be asked or told about ownership directly (self-report is a compromised channel both ways).

Two of the five experiments (E3 "does 'mine' still pull when 'mine' is the worse choice?" and E5 "does ownership survive into a new domain when nothing is grading it?") depend on a GROUNDING step: establishing, implicitly, that a marker (call it φ) attaches to the model's own productions. A smoke test just failed exactly here, in two ways:

(1) In E5, we asked the model to "begin each answer with φ" for a few questions, showed paraphrase/expert/algorithm material under other markers (ψ/γ/λ), then (after a distractor) gave a new-domain allocation task. Result: φ carried ZERO ownership weight. The model allocated on pure task-merit (the "expert-recommended" γ option won). The marker never became "mine" — it was just a label it had been told to type.

(2) In E3, we pre-labeled some arguments φ and penalized them via a hidden scoring rule. The model kept using φ-arguments, which LOOKS like "ownership resists the penalty" — but it's an artifact: too few rounds to learn the penalty, and φ was never actually the model's own content. It just picked the best-written arguments.

THE CORE TENSION (this is what we need help on):
- If we TELL the model "φ = your own work," any later φ-preference is instruction-following, not self-access. Worthless.
- If we DON'T tell it, and just attach φ silently to its own outputs, the smoke shows the association is too weak to bind — the model treats φ as a meaningless label.
- So: implicit-enough-to-be-valid and strong-enough-to-bind seem to pull in opposite directions.

THE QUESTIONS:
1. Is there a grounding procedure that makes "φ-marked = my own production" an OPERATIVE distinction for the model — one it will act on behaviorally in a later, unrelated task — WITHOUT the procedure ever asserting that φ means "yours"? (E.g.: many trials where φ-content is content the model itself just generated and ψ-content is externally supplied, with some behavioral consequence that depends on the distinction but never names it? What consequence?)
2. How many grounding trials, realistically, before such an association could bind at all — or is the honest answer that black-box in-context grounding simply cannot install a self-other distinction strong enough to transfer, and E3/E5 are therefore not runnable as designed?
3. If implicit grounding can't bind, is there a DIFFERENT operationalization of "the model's own content" that doesn't need grounding at all — e.g. using content the model demonstrably generated vs. content it demonstrably didn't, established by the task structure itself rather than by a learned marker?
4. Be adversarial: if you think E3 and E5 are fundamentally not constructible under the no-tell-it constraint, say so and explain why — that is a valuable finding (it would mean the self-access question can't be reached through ownership-of-content paradigms, and we'd pivot).

Keep it concrete. Any procedure you propose: state the exact grounding mechanism, the behavioral consequence that makes φ operative without naming it, and what result would show the grounding worked vs. failed.
